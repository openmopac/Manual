Note about Force Constants

When a geometry is defined in Cartesian coordinates in a FORCE calculation, force constants for each atom are printed near the bottom of the output.  These force constants are the second derivatives of the displacement along the directions x, y, and z in millidynes per Ångstrom.  When sets of equivalent atoms are present, you might expect that the force constants for a specific coordinate might be the same.  In some molecules, such as ethylene, C2H4, this is true, but in other species, ethane, C2H6, for example, some force constants for equivalent atoms are different.  Why is this?   To understand what's happening we will use ethane as the example.  Here is the data set for ethane, a system with D3d symmetry:

 The force matrix is used in calculating the normal modes or vibrational frequencies, and even a cursory inspection of these shows that the symmetries of the normal modes is correct, therefore by implication, the force constant matrix has the correct symmetry, even though it might not be obvious.  So what's happening? Look at the diagonal terms; these represent the force-constants for the atoms.  In particular, elements (4,4), (7,7), and (10,10) are the x-axis force-constants for H1, H2, and H3, respectively.  If the force-constants were orthogonal, then the off-diagonal terms within each atom would be zero.  In this example here they are non-zero, indicating that the orthogonal force-constants do not point along the Cartesian axes.  This means that the components of the force-constant along the three axes is x-axis is not uniquely defined.  The only quantity that is uniquely defined is the addition of the three force constants components, here (4.11474 + 0.62412 + 0.70577) = 5.44463 = (0.90799 + 3.83087 + 0.70577) = (0.90798 1.19839 3.33826).

In order to obtain the unique orthogonal force constants for an atom, the small 3x3 matrix of force constants would need to be diagonalized. If that were to be done, then the three associated eigenvalues would represent the force constants, and the corresponding eigenvectors would give the direction of these force constants.  However, this quantity is not of much use.  An alternative quantity would be the force constants for the internal coordinates. Now the bond-stretch force constants for hydrogen are all the same. But note - although the angle and dihedral force constants might be expected to be the same for all the hydrogen atoms, they are in fact different.  This is a manifestation of the same problem that was found when Cartesian coordinates were used - the orthogonal force constants do not point along the internal coordinates.  When internal coordinates are used, an even more severe conceptual problem is encountered when force constants for cyclic systems, such as benzene, are calculated.  For benzene, the force constants in internal coordinates are:
