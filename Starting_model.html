Starting Model for Chymotrypsin Catalytic Mechanism

The starting model for investigating the catalytic process whereby chymotrypsin catalyzes the hydrolysis will now be described.  As this is a quite complicated model, before proceeding further, an introduction to some of the features of this model will now be given. 

How this picture was made

The starting point for this picture was the PDB file 8GCH.  8GCH consists of four chains, E: residues 1-10, F: residues 16-146, G: residues 150-245, and C: residues 250:252.  In addition, there are four sulfate ions and 341 water molecules. The substrate is chain C, and consists of the sequence Gly250-Ala251-Trp252; Gly ended with -NH2, and Trp ended with -COOH.   This substrate is the product of peptide hydrolysis by the active site.  To the starting model, hydrogen atoms were added, residue Asp102 ionized, and the resulting geometry optimized using PM7.  The model was made more realistic by using implicit solvation (COSMO).  To generate a hydrolysable substrate the carboxylic acid group, -COOH, in chain C was replaced by -CO-NH-CH3.  As a result of all these operations, the starting model consisted of the fully-optimized geometry of chymotrypsin in water with a small peptide docked in the active site.

Formula: The starting model has the formula C1112 H2446 N301 O703 S16 or 4,578 atoms, and has a net charge of minus one, i.e. [C1112 H2446 N301 O703 S16]-.   Modeling enzyme mechanisms is simplified if the formula of the system being used is a constant, so for all work relating to the chymotrypsin mechanism the formula will be a constant as shown here.

Interesting features of the starting model

(A) The catalytic triad: Residues His57, Asp102, and Ser195.  These form the classic charge relay system.

(B) The substrate: In the starting model, this is H-Gly250-Ala251-Trp252-NH-CH3.

(C) The oxyanion hole: Residues Gly193-Asp194-Ser195. Asp194 is ionized, and forms a salt bridge with Ile16. The peptide hydrogen of Gly193 forms a hydrogen bond (length 1.81Å) with the carbonyl oxygen of Trp252.  This oxygen also hydrogen bonds (length 2.02Å) to the peptide hydrogen of Ser195.  The third possible hydrogen bond, to Asp194, does not form because its peptide hydrogen atom points away from the carbonyl oxygen.

(D) Ser214: The hydroxyl of Ser214 forms a strong hydrogen bond (1.59Å) to the carboxylate ion of Asp102, and its carbonyl oxygen hydrogen bonds to the hydroxyl hydrogen of Ser195, and also hydrogen bonds to the amide hydrogen of Trp252.  This residue is unusual, in that in the original PDB structure, it is too far from Trp252 to hydrogen bond, however the distance from Trp252 in various PDB files varies over a wide range.  The hydrogen bond to Ser195 is also interesting in that it locks the orientation of the hydroxyl in the triad serine.

(E) Salt bridges: Salt bridges exist between Glu21 and Arg154 (length 2.44Å), Glu20 and Ser11 (1.40Å), and between Ile16 and Asp194 (1.56Å).  His57 forms a strong hydrogen bond (1.61Å) with Asp102, but because His57 is not ionized in the starting model, this cannot be called a salt bridge.
