Identifying and exploring the Active site

The active site - the part of an enzyme where covalent bonds are made or broken - is the most important part of the entire system. A knowledge of the features of the active site is essential for understanding the mechanism of catalysis.  Using a GUI, locate the active site - this is probably best done using residue numbers - then look for features in the vicinity of the active site that might be of interest.

For example, using 1AFQ, a chymotrypsin protease with a synthetic inhibitor, the active site consists of His57, Asp102, and Ser195, all on chain "B".  Near these three residues, known as the catalytic triad, are a water molecule, HOH 603, and the inhibitor, 4-fluorobenzylamine, FBA D 303.  If the process of preparing a starting model had been carried out, then the Asp will be ionized, there will be a hydrogen atom attached to a nitrogen atom of the imidazole ring, pointing towards the Asp(-), and the Ser alcohol hydrogen will be hydrogen bonding to the other nitrogen atom on the imidazole ring.

Explore the active site

When the instructions for preparing a starting model are used with 1AFQ, the resulting structure is the classical active site for 1AFQ.  In this structure, the Asp102 is ionized, His57 is neutral, and the Ser195 hydrogen-bonds to the imidazole ring nitrogen.
