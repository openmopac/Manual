Hydrogenation of biologic macromolecules using MOPAC

Two conditions are necessary for protein modeling: a computational chemistry technique, and a realistic model. Most models are constructed from PDB files, which normally do not include hydrogen atoms.  Several methods are available for adding hydrogen atoms, of which readily available methods are MolProbity, WHAT IF, GOLD, and HAAD Online.

So why have another hydrogenation routine?  For computational chemistry modeling work, a realistic model must comply with very strict rules, for example:

  • The model must be for the system of interest.  Such systems might include metal ions and heterogroups such as heme and porphyrin.

  • Hydrogenation must be correct.  Even one atom incorrectly hydrogenated could invalidate the model.

  • Selective protonation to form salt bridges, isolated ionized sites, etc., must be allowed.

In MOPAC, hydrogenation is done in two steps.  In the first step, using keyword ADD-H, hydrogen atoms are added to neutralize as much of the system as possible.  Moieties that always exist as ions, for example sodium and calcium, are not hydrogenated.  This produces a well-defined starting point for the second step.

In the second step, keyword SITE is used to form ionized sites.  These are the salt bridges and individual ionized residues and other species.

To minimize errors, both steps are normally done in one job by having ADD-H, and one or more SITE keywords.

When hydrogen atoms are added to a system, they are not well positioned.  Before proceeding, therefore, a geometry optimization of the hydrogen atoms needs to be performed.  A recommended procedure for this operation would be to include the CHARGE keyword.   If the net charge on the system is known, then that should be used.  Thus simple proteins, hydrogenated using ADD-H SITE=(SALT), should have a charge of zero. If the calculated charge is different to that expected, examine the output to locate the cause of the difference.  Assigning the net charge can be extremely tedious when large proteins are used.  To simplify this operation, a list of all charged sites is printed whenever an SCF calculation is requested. When SITE is used, a list of the changes in charged sites is printed, together with the net change in charge. 

Non-zero net charges can exist when metal atoms, such as Na and Ca, are present, also when complicated neutral heterogroups, such as heme, are covalently bound to proteins.  This most commonly occurs when Fe of heme forms a bond with the sulfur atom of a cysteine residue.

MOZYME will not allow a system to be run where the net charge defined by a keyword is different to that calculated by MOZYME.  If MOZYME does make a mistake, typically by picking the wrong resonance structure, the fault can be corrected by keywords such as SETPI or, less commonly, by CVB.
