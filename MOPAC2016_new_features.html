New Features and Improved Features in MOPAC2016

A new version of MOPAC will be released in early 2016; this version will be named MOPAC2016.  There will be increased focus on biochemical applications, in particular in modeling proteins.  Although MOPAC2012 was able to model biomolecules efficiently, its use for this task was limited by the difficulties involved in constructing data-sets.  In particular, there were only a small number of tools in MOPAC2012 for manipulating PDB files or structures resulting from MOPAC calculations.

The new MOPAC2016 will have several new tools for examining, comparing, and modifying macromolecules; some will be ready when MOPAC2016 is released, others are still being written.  These tools should make research into biochemical processes using MOPAC much easier, and, in turn, should result in a greater usage of the large compute engine in MOPAC.  Several worked examples were used during the development of these tools in order to find weaknesses in the procedures.  As a result, the new methods for manipulating systems have become more robust and intuitive.

PDB Operations

Automatic conversion of a raw PDB file into a MOPAC data set. Any existing hydrogen atoms are removed and new hydrogen atoms added, then the atoms are re-sequenced to put them into standard PDB form.

When hydrogen atoms added or deleted the system is re-sequenced so that the hydrogen atoms are in their correct place in the data-set.

Automatic re-arrangement of atoms when comparing pairs of PDB structures.  When two files that represent the same structure are compared, the atoms in the second file are re-arranged so as to minimize the RMS difference.  An option exists to allow structures that have some, but not all, atoms in common to be compared.

Three new metrics are provided when comparing structures.  (1) Individual atoms that are in different positions in the two structures are identified along with the distance between the two positions. A maximum of 30 such atoms are reported. (2) Interatomic distances that are different in the two structures are printed.  A maximum of 25 distances are reported. (3) Hydrogen bonds present in one structure but not in the other, together with H-bond length and the energy, are printed.  All such hydrogen bonds that are less than 3.0 Å and have a stabilization greater than 0.8 kcal.mol-1 are printed.

New global properties for comparing structures are: (A) The total non-covalent energies. (B) The number of hydrogen bonds common to both systems. (C) The total, average, and RMS difference between the two geometries.

A JSmol web-page can be written that allows a detailed comparison to be made of pairs of structures.
